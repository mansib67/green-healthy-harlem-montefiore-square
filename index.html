Project overview

Project title: Green and Healthy Harlem: Redesigning Montefiore Square for shade, comfort, planting, and public well-being.

Project goal: improve the plaza as a healthier public space with stronger tree canopy, better daily seating choice, cooler walking paths, and a clearer wellness identity.

Essential question: How can Montefiore Square become greener, more comfortable, and more inviting to stay in without losing the structure people already recognize?

The design does not treat the site as empty. It treats the site as already improved but still incomplete in comfort terms. The strongest opportunity is to push shade, softer planting layers, and healthier everyday use much further.

Problem definition and site context

Montefiore Square already has lawn, paving, storefront edges, benches, and young trees. The problem is not that the site lacks structure. The problem is that the plaza still reads more as a route through space than a place designed for long comfort.

  • Shade: main walking lines and sitting zones still depend on trees that have not matured enough.
  • Seating choice: fixed benches do not offer enough variety for privacy, group size, or preferred sun and shade.
  • Planting depth: greener edges are needed at eye level, not only as lawn at ground level.
  • Wellness identity: the plaza needs clearer cues for walking, pausing, resting, and evening use.

The redesign therefore focuses on comfort, cooling, greenery, and healthier daily use rather than on erasing the site and starting from zero.

Comfort and climate analysis

The brief for Green and Healthy Harlem asks for a comfort and climate analysis plus a shade and seating study. The main findings are simple: more canopy is needed over the most used paths, planting should work harder as climate infrastructure, and sitting should feel flexible instead of fixed.

Midday shade

Primary routes and the central paved zones still feel exposed before the canopy fully matures.

Seating choice

Most sitting is fixed, so users cannot easily choose stronger shade, social spacing, or privacy.

Planting needs

Bioswale edges, understory planting, and seasonal meadow layers can cool and soften the public room.

Healthy use

A plaza that supports walking loops, pause space, quiet retreat, and evening comfort can invite longer stays.

Three concept options

The handbook requires three design options before the final choice. These studies test different priorities while keeping the same site and project brief in view. The goal was not to invent three random looks for the plaza. The goal was to test three different ideas of what healthier public-space performance could mean here, then choose the one that best fits Montefiore Square itself.

Option 1 · Shade First Plaza

What it prioritizes: immediate thermal relief. This version concentrates new canopy, pergolas, and shaded seating over the routes and pause areas that already carry the most daily use.

Who it serves best: commuters moving through the square during hot weather, seniors who need predictable shade to stay in the plaza at all, and families with children who cannot remain long in direct sun.

Strongest contribution: it would likely produce the fastest measurable comfort gain in summer because it addresses the site’s clearest weakness first: exposed paving and limited midday shade.

What it sacrifices: by focusing so strongly on heat relief, it risks becoming a better shaded pass-through rather than a fuller wellness destination with reasons to return beyond sitting in shade.

Option 2 · Green Wellness Plaza

What it prioritizes: balance. This option combines stronger canopy, layered planting, a measured wellness loop, flexible seating, quieter retreat space, and calmer all-day use without overloading the plaza with one single program identity.

Who it serves best: mixed users such as seniors, hospital staff, patients and visitors, families, nearby residents, and commuters who sometimes want to pass through quickly and sometimes want to stay.

Strongest contribution: it improves comfort, greenery, circulation, and wellness identity at the same time. Instead of solving only one problem well, it makes the whole plaza perform better across the full day.

What it sacrifices: it depends on coordination across planting, maintenance, furniture management, and light programming, so its full value comes from steady operations rather than one dramatic design move.

Selected final concept

Option 3 · Active Healthy Plaza

What it prioritizes: movement and visible health programming. Fitness zones, yoga space, and a stronger exercise-focused loop make activity the main identity of the plaza.

Who it serves best: health-oriented adults, organized community groups, and users looking for exercise or scheduled outdoor programming rather than quiet everyday rest.

Strongest contribution: it has the highest potential for recurring programmed use and strong community ownership because it gives people a reason to come back at a set time for a repeated activity.

What it sacrifices: it can feel too narrow for a plaza that also needs to welcome quiet sitting, short breaks, recovery, and casual pass-through use. Without regular programming support, some of its infrastructure could feel underused.

Option 2 was selected because it sits between the other two extremes in the most useful way. It keeps the urgent shade logic of Option 1 and the wellness identity of Option 3, but it does not turn Montefiore Square into either a one-purpose shade field or a heavily programmed activity plaza. For this site, next to Montefiore Medical Center and used by many different kinds of people, the most convincing solution is the one that improves comfort and health while still leaving the square open, calm, and flexible.

Final redesign proposal

The selected Green Wellness Plaza keeps Montefiore Square recognizable while making comfort and health legible in every part of the site experience. The redesign does not erase the site and replace it with a fantasy plaza. It starts from the existing lawn, paths, benches, edges, and young canopy, then strengthens each of those parts so the square reads less like a route through space and more like a place designed for staying, recovering, and returning.

Arrival: entering the redesigned plaza should feel different immediately. Reinforced canopy, planted edge bands, and clearer threshold moments at the entries signal that this is not just exposed leftover paving. The first visible moves are shade, greenery, and softer borders, so the experience begins with a sense of entering an intentional public room.

Circulation: the wellness loop is treated as real infrastructure rather than a decorative path. Subtle surface distinction, directional arrows, and regular distance markers help it support two kinds of movement at once: fast daily pass-through circulation and intentional short walks for stress relief, recovery, or light exercise. Rest points along the route make the loop usable for seniors, hospital visitors, and anyone who needs movement with pause moments built in.

Pause zones: moveable chairs and café tables are distributed in small and medium clusters instead of being lined up in one fixed pattern. That gives users real choice: two people can sit together in conversation, a family can gather around a table, and a single person can choose a quieter edge seat without being isolated from the larger public space.

Retreat zone: one of the most important changes is the quieter meditation-garden area set slightly off the main flow. This part of the plaza is planted more densely, with softer ground-level texture and stronger shade, so it feels calmer than the exposed central pavement. It is designed for people who need a break, including patients, hospital staff, older adults, and anyone looking for a short period of quiet within a busy neighborhood setting.

Evening: after sunset, the plaza should still feel calm and usable instead of fading into darkness or harsh glare. Warm low-level path lighting, light in the tree canopy, and clearer visibility at seating areas support safer evening use while preserving a softer public atmosphere. The result is a plaza that remains active later in the day without becoming loud or overlit.

The design is intentionally balanced. It does not overprogram every square foot, but it clearly supports walking, sitting, cooling down, recovering, and coming back. That balance is why it is the strongest fit for Montefiore Square.

Budget, timeline, and maintenance

These numbers are conceptual student estimates for phasing and discussion. They are not a final construction bid. Estimate ranges are informed by comparable NYC Parks and DEP green-infrastructure project scales plus classroom-level cost benchmarking.

Climate, comfort, and existing-condition evidence

  • Urban heat: dense paved areas in New York can run substantially hotter than greener surroundings during summer afternoons; this supports a shade-first canopy strategy.
  • Shade timing: review images indicate key exposure windows around 8:00 AM, 12:00 PM, 3:00 PM, and 6:00 PM, with strongest comfort stress near midday and early afternoon.
  • Seating gap: existing seating is mostly fixed and limited in arrangement choice; flexible furniture addresses both capacity and user-type mismatch.
  • Canopy goal: concept target moves from an estimated 10–15% current canopy influence toward ~40% long-term shaded coverage as trees establish.

Community and equity layer

Montefiore Square sits next to Montefiore Medical Center, making hospital staff, patients, and visitors a core user group for a wellness-oriented plaza. The redesign also serves seniors, families, commuters, and health-focused adults through ADA-priority circulation, visible and predictable shade, seating with varied support levels, and multilingual wayfinding appropriate for neighborhood demographics.

The proposal is designed as both infrastructure and partnership opportunity: Montefiore health programming, NYC Parks stewardship, and local community organization events can activate the space without changing the underlying layout.

Implementation timeline

  1. Phase 1: add moveable furniture, temporary planting, and walking-loop markers to create visible low-cost wins and test daily use patterns.
  2. Phase 2: plant additional trees and install bioswales and understory edges early so canopy and stormwater systems can establish over multiple seasons.
  3. Phase 3: complete lighting upgrades and finalize long-term operations based on observed circulation and evening-use behavior from Phases 1 and 2.

Funding pathway (conceptual)

  • NYC Parks capital allocations and neighborhood plaza improvement programs.
  • NYC DEP green-infrastructure funding for bioswale and runoff-management components.
  • Borough-level discretionary capital support.
  • Hospital-community benefit funding tied to public-health outcomes near Montefiore Medical Center.

Maintenance note

The healthiest version of the plaza depends on regular watering during plant establishment, seasonal pruning and replanting, litter control around seating clusters, and an operations plan for moveable furniture (daily distribution, nighttime storage, and accountability).

Planting strategy by layer (named species)

  • Canopy trees: London Plane (Platanus × acerifolia), Honeylocust (Gleditsia triacanthos), and Swamp White Oak (Quercus bicolor).
  • Understory and meadow edge: Switchgrass (Panicum virgatum), Little Bluestem (Schizachyrium scoparium), Black-eyed Susan (Rudbeckia hirta), Purple Coneflower (Echinacea purpurea), and Inkberry Holly (Ilex glabra).
  • Bioswale planting: native sedges (Carex spp.), Blue Flag Iris (Iris versicolor), and Joe-Pye Weed (Eutrochium purpureum) for wet-dry tolerance.

Wellness-loop details

The proposed loop is planned as a measured path (targeting a short repeatable walk length), with directional arrows, distance markers at regular intervals, and rest points that pair benches with shaded planting pockets. Surface treatment is intended to be visually distinct and gentler underfoot than adjacent hardscape where feasible.

AI workflow and human judgment

The handbook says students should begin with real site views, use those views as visual references, generate multiple options, compare them, and select the strongest versions. That exact screenshot-based workflow is used here.

Step 1

Collect real Google Maps screenshots from multiple Montefiore Square angles.

Step 2

Generate redesign studies for aerial structure, shaded corridors, meditation zones, market variation, night comfort, and active healthy use.

Step 3

Compare each real screenshot with generated outputs, then reject any image that drifts from the original camera angle, changes site geometry, or breaks the Group 5 brief.

Step 4

Keep the images that best support shade, comfort, planting, and public well-being.

AI helped with exploration, but the final ordering, concept selection, captions, and project framing stayed human. The process deliberately rejected outputs that looked generic, drifted from Montefiore Square geometry, exaggerated the scale of the site, or introduced expensive visual drama that did not match the actual brief.

That filtering step mattered as much as the generation step. The most useful AI studies were not the flashiest ones. They were the images that stayed close to the real site, preserved the camera viewpoint, and made believable changes in shade, planting, furniture, and atmosphere.

Human design judgment determined the final balance: Green Wellness Plaza was selected over the more extreme shade-only and activity-only options because it better serves mixed daily users and supports both self-directed and organized wellness use. If this project were pushed further, the next step would be a more detailed on-site sun study and a more exact measured loop diagram tied to real circulation distances.

Reflection, success metrics, and contact form

The final concept was chosen because it fits the Green and Healthy Harlem brief better than a purely market-focused or purely activity-focused plaza. It improves comfort without losing Montefiore Square’s recognizable layout, and it turns the strongest parts of the site into a clearer argument about shade, health, and everyday public use.

The larger lesson of the project is that Montefiore Square does not need total reinvention. It needs a more deliberate version of what is already beginning to work: stronger canopy, deeper planting, more seating choice, clearer wellness cues, and a more visible connection to the people who use the site every day, especially users connected to Montefiore Medical Center.
